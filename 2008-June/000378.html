I use Linux for much of my development work.  The contributions I make
are in the areas of weak-signal algorithms and their implementation, not
in problems related to an updated OS or other system software.

I want the resulting programs to be useful to many people, so I produce
installable packages that run without problems on Windows.  I make sure
that the programs also run without problems on my Linux installations.

Others have graciously pitched in to make installable *nix packages,
which is great.  These packages generally work fine ... until you break
something by installing some "latest update" on your machine.

As I said, if you want to play computers, OK ...; but if you want to
have a stable base on which to run a complex program with many demands
on the OS, you'll need to (1) operate differently, (2) learn to fix what
your updates broke, or (3) be patient and wait for someone else to find
time to address your problem.

Among the WSJT, MAP65, and WSPR users I know about who run the programs
on one flavor or another of *nix, most seem quite ablt to keep the
program running smoothly and to be happy with their choice or OS.
